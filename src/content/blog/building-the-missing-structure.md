The first thing I ever built was a marketplace that did not exist yet. It ran out of my locker, in the second grade.\
\
The school's bookshop only sold the fun trinkets students wanted once a month, as a special event, and the rest of the time there was nowhere to get the small strange stuff students actually wanted to play with: origami, magnets, the random collectables of the time that kids would trade. So I built a place that filled the gap: I sourced the goods, stocked the locker, managed the collaborators, and kept it running between the special events. I did not think of it as a pattern at the time. It was just the work in front of me.

It turned out to be a pattern.

In high school the missing piece was connection itself; a few hundred students passing each other between bells with nothing to belong to; no shared culture, no rivalry worth the name, no reason a freshman and a senior would ever know each other. So I worked with the administration to build out a house system and ran it through everything that made the school a school: orientation, homerooms, clubs, schedules, field day until belonging and cooperation were part of the place instead of left to chance.

At university, working as the Student Body President, I again noticed a lack. Underneath the title there was little to no real structure; no working system, no consistency to allow for progress from class to class, just another class year coming into a new environment waiting on someone to make it mean something. So before I could do the job, I had to build the thing that made the job possible.

A few years later I was at a chemicals company older than the light bulb, and I founded its North American Government Affairs function from nothing; no team, no mandate, no precedent to copy, just a gap that mattered and the room to build into it. Lately the gap has been my own: I built an autonomous AI system to run the operations of how I work, because the tool I actually wanted did not exist yet, and waiting for someone else to ship it felt like the slower path. That makes five, each the same move at a different scale.

I diverge; the point is this. There are two ways to move through most institutions. You can inherit the structure and optimize inside it, or you can notice the structure is not there and build it. I have never been very good at the first one. I reach, almost involuntarily, for the system underneath the goal: the process that makes the next ten goals cheaper, and the one after that nearly free.

Though building the missing structure is slower than borrowing one, and it rarely arrives with permission, it compounds in a way borrowed structure never does, because you understand every load-bearing piece; you were the one who set them.

This site is the same move, one more time.

Most portfolios are showrooms: a polished record of finished things, sealed behind glass, accurate the day it shipped and a little more wrong every week after. I wanted a workshop instead; something that grows as I do, shows the reasoning and not only the result, and stays honest about what did not work. You will find the systems I am building (an AI operations framework, an AgTech platform, a handful of smaller experiments), writing on strategy and technology and the seam between them, and the occasional post-mortem on a good idea that did not survive contact with reality.

If one framework here saves you a month, or one honest mistake spares you from making it yourself, then the structure was worth building.

I have always held that entrepreneurship is having the vision of a better future, the creativity to design a bridge between it and the present, and the passion to build that bridge so others can cross it trustingly. So this is me, building in the open, one beam at a time.

And if you are standing in a room that does not quite exist yet: what are you still waiting for permission to build?
